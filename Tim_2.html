Tim Berners-Lee: Inventor of the World Wide Web

About Gallery

Tim Berners-Lee is a British computer scientist who invented the World Wide Web in 1989. He wrote the first web browser in 1990 while employed at CERN near Geneva, Switzerland.

Early Life

Berners-Lee was born in London, England, and grew up in Beckenham, Kent. He attended Sheen Mount Primary School in London, and then went on to study at Emanuel School in Battersea, London. He studied physics at The Queen's College, Oxford, from 1973 to 1976.

Career

Berners-Lee worked as a software engineer at CERN from 1980 to 1984. He proposed a global hypertext project, which became the World Wide Web. He also wrote the first web server, browser, and editor in 1990. In 1991, he implemented the first successful communication between an HTTP client and server via the internet.

Awards and Honors

Berners-Lee has received numerous awards and honors for his invention of the World Wide Web, including the 2016 Turing Award, the 2007 Japan Prize, the 2002 Prince of Asturias Award, the 2001 Charles Babbage Award, the 1999 W.W. McDowell Award, and the 1995 IEEE Koji Kobayashi Computers and Communications Award.
